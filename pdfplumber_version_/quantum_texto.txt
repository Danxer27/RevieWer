1 Introduction
Quantum mechanics is a branch of physics that describes the behaviour of matter and
energy at, and below, the atomic scale [1]. Quantum computing builds upon these prin-
ciples by harnessing the unique properties of quantum systems—such as superposi-
tion and entanglement—to perform computations at the nanoscale [2]. These methods
enable extraordinary processing power by exploiting the probabilistic nature of quantum
phenomena [2].

2 An overview on quantum computing
The role of quantum computation in complex problem-solving is grounded in the prin-
ciples of quantum mechanics and computational theory. The fundamental elements of
quantum computing—qubits, superposition, and entanglement—enable information to
be represented and processed in ways that transcend the limitations of classical compu-
tation. These quantum phenomena offer a glimpse into a new era of computation that
promises exponential improvements in processing power and efficiency.

both 0 and 1 simultaneously. This unique property allows quantum computers to per-

2.4 Quantum algorithms
Quantum algorithms are specifically designed to exploit the distinctive properties of
quantum computing. These include Shor’s, Grover’s, Simon’s, Bernstein–Vazirani, and
Deutsch–Jozsa algorithms, which provide either quadratic or exponential speed-up for
specific computational tasks. Among them, the most well-known and experimentally
demonstrated are Shor’s [34] and Grover’s [35] algorithms. These quantum approaches
utilize interference to amplify correct solutions and suppress incorrect ones, thereby
accelerating the overall problem-solving process.

2.5 Quantum computing challenges
Quantum computing is a rapidly advancing field that offers numerous advantages. Yet,
it faces significant scientific, engineering and practical challenges, as illustrated in Fig. 7.
Quantum hardware is still experimental, requires extreme low temperature and is prone
to errors and decoherence. In addition, limited practical algorithms, noise resilience,
implementation complexity, high cost and manufacturing issues are included.
Despite, the setback researchers are working on mitigation strategies such as better
qubit materials, photonic interconnects, noise tolerant and hybrid algorithms. Similarly,
efficient QEC codes, logical and topological qubits, cloud access and cryogenic electron-
ics are ways to improve quantum computing.
2.5.1 Decoherence
Decoherence refers to the sensitivity of qubits to their surrounding environment;
these interactions cause a gradual decay of quantum behaviour until it eventually dis-
appears [39]. In mesoscopic systems such as qubit devices, decoherence poses a major
obstacle to maintaining quantum coherence. However, techniques such as error cor-
rection, environmental shielding, and dynamical decoupling are employed to enhance

2.5.2 Error correction
The necessity for error correction is higher for quantum computing than for classical
computation. Error correction in quantum world is more difficult for some reasons [30]
1. There no-cloning theorem.
2. It has continuous or infinitely several different possible errors.
3. Measurement test of a correct state can collapse the state resulting to loss of

Quantum error-correcting codes encode a single logical qubit into a larger number of
physical qubits, allowing errors affecting some of them to be detected and corrected.
Fault-tolerant computation represents another essential strategy, designed to prevent
the propagation of errors during quantum operations and to ensure the reliability of
large-scale quantum systems.
2.5.3 Algorithm and software
Classical probabilistic algorithms can often be simulated efficiently using quantum algo-
rithms. However, the reverse is generally not true—classical probabilistic methods can-
not efficiently simulate quantum algorithms. Replacing quantum gates with probabilistic
classical gates yields different outcomes and typically fails to reproduce genuine quan-
tum behavior [29].
Many quantum algorithms leverage the unique properties of quantum computation
to achieve superior performance. Nevertheless, there remains an urgent need to design
and develop new quantum algorithms, a process that requires a deeper understanding
of quantum mechanics and its translation into practical algorithmic frameworks. Simi-
larly, developing quantum programming languages presents an ongoing challenge, as the
programming paradigms and logic structures underlying quantum computation differ
fundamentally from those used in classical computing.

2.5.4 Hardware and infrastructures
Quantum computers are more susceptible to errors than classical digital computers
because quantum–mechanical systems are inherently sensitive and difficult to control
[29]. Thus, scalability remains one of the major challenges in quantum computing. Build-
ing large-scale quantum computers with a sufficient number of qubits continues to pose
a significant technical obstacle [14]. The performance of these systems is constrained by
factors such as qubit connectivity, coherence time, and gate fidelity. Understanding and
addressing these challenges is imperative for the continued advancement and practical
implementation of quantum-computing technologies.
2.6 Hybrid quantum computing
Although there are significant challenges as the quantum computing landscape evolves,
a wide range of intertwined possibilities would be established that would lead to a future
of limitless possibilities. Harnessing classical and quantum computing into a hybrid sys-
tem can create interesting quantum algorithms, such as a hybrid intelligent quantum
algorithm [40, 41]. As an example, quantum computing provides an efficient improve-
ment to Prim’s algorithm [42]. Hybrid quantum neural networks enable advantages in
optimisation and simulation fields [43]. When quantum convolutions are carried out,
the results obtained are fed into a classical neural network to achieve a quantum–clas-
sical hybrid model [44]. Hybrid CNN performance converges faster than the quantum
version, as Fig. 8 illustrates.
Similarly, Ganesan et al. [45] synergise a combination of quantum computing, evolu-
tionary algorithm, meta-heuristic and multi-objective techniques to create quantum-
inspired meta-heuristic(QIMH). The approach solves hard, comprehensive problems to
obtain excellent optimisation. Indeed, hybrid algorithms would provide computational
speed and a quality solution. This also mitigates some challenges associated with quan-
tum computing.

3.1 Transportation application
Transportation network design is a well-studied problem concerned with the planning
and operation of transportation systems. It is widely applied to identify new roadway
links and to enhance existing network efficiency. Dixit and Niu [47] utilized quantum
computing to address design problems in transportation networks, demonstrating sig-
nificant computational benefits achieved through quantum annealing. Wang et al.
[48] further illustrated how the advantages of quantum computing can be leveraged to
improve existing intelligent transportation systems (ITS).
However, Cooper [49] explored the potential applications of quantum computation in
transportation modelling and concluded that implementing quantum algorithms may
remain challenging due to the space overheads imposed by the requirements of revers-
ible computation.

3.3 Geotechnical application
Soil properties are complex and difficult to establish an effective surface response for
traditional slope stability. Xu et al. [60], developed an intelligent optimisation algorithm
by combining the forest regression method(RF) and an improved quantum genetic algo-
rithm. The method identified a critical slip surface for a slope. The distinct properties
of soil layers and fluctuation of the bearing stratum is a major challenge in the design
of pile foundation. Yin et al. [61], established a BP neural network to predict evaluat-
ing samples, then proposed a quantum particle swarm optimization(QPSO) algorithm
to optimise weights and thresholds of the BP network. The authors compared the results
obtained from QPSO-BP with 3 robust models, FWA-BP, PSO-BP and BP. The accuracy
of QPSO-BP was high, which showed superiority amongst the models.

3.4 Hydraulic system
The optimal placement of sensors is key to detecting and isolating faults in Water Dis-
tribution Networks (WDNs), which represents a large-scale combinatorial optimization
problem. Speziali et al. [63] presented a detailed procedure to address this challenge
using adiabatic quantum computation.
Meng and Yang [64] proposed a quantum computing framework based on hydrody-
namic Schrodinger equation (HSE) to simulate 3D turbulent flows in different engineer-
ing applications. The authors concluded that 3D turbulent structures and statistics have
a promising feat in CFD implementation.
The mechanical and physical properties of hydraulic structures evolve over time due
to complex operational conditions. Zhu et al. [65] implemented a Quantum Genetic
Algorithm (QGA) to inversely analyze the partitioned deformation modulus of high arch
dams. The authors concluded that the QGA demonstrated excellent global search capa-
bility and population diversity. Furthermore, the feasibility and effectiveness of the algo-
rithm were validated through practical engineering applications.
3.5 Construction management
One major benefit of quantum computing in construction management is its ability
to automate complex optimization processes. Oke et al. [66] outlined steps toward the
implementation of quantum computing for sustainable infrastructure management. The
combination of quantum computing and artificial intelligence (AI) can provide excep-
tional benefits to construction management by improving efficiency, predictive analysis,
and sustainability outcomes.
3.6 Environmental
Environmental engineering plays a crucial role in ensuring the health and well-being
of society as well as the environment. New strategies and technologies are continually
developed to protect the planet and its ecosystems. Ballari et al. [67] investigated the
potential of quantum computation for optimizing environmental engineering processes.
The authors concluded that Quantum Computing (QC) offers an innovative and high-
speed method for solving complex environmental problems.
Similarly, Arora and Kumar [68] emphasised the importance of environmental sus-
tainability through quantum technologies. They proposed a Carbon-Aware Quantum
Computing (CQC) framework and called for a new research direction known as sustain-
able quantum computing, which aims to minimise the environmental footprint of future
quantum systems.

3.7 Damage detection
Damage assessment of buildings following an earthquake is often a labour-intensive,
time-consuming, and error-prone process when using traditional methods. However,
Quantum Neural Networks (QNNs) have been developed to predict seismic damage
more efficiently [69]. The Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is a well-known algo-
rithm for seismic damage prediction, although it can suffer from delays or inaccuracies
in damage evaluation.
Bhatta and Dang [70] presented a novel Quantum Convolutional Neural Network
(QCNN) technique to detect damage in reinforced concrete buildings after an earth-
quake. The QCNN model was trained using damaged images of reinforced concrete
structures from previous earthquakes. The seismic damage detection accuracy obtained
from the QCNN model was compared with results from traditional CNN architectures,
demonstrating improved performance.
For large-scale datasets and faster computational speed, Quantum-Enhanced Machine
Learning (QML) offers significant advantages over Classical Machine Learning (CML)
[71]. Similarly, Liu [72] proposed a Quantum-Behaviour Particle Swarm Optimisation
(QPSO) algorithm to enhance the detection of damage in civil structures. The approach
combined a wavelet transform threshold clustering algorithm, neural-network adaptive
learning, and Monte Carlo simulation, with the inclusion of Support Vector Machine
(SVM) regression and random sequence prediction. Experimental results showed that
the minimum wavelet detection error reached 2.4%, indicating that the algorithm pro-
vides significant potential for civil infrastructure damage detection.
3.8 Corrosion
Corrosion remains a major issue affecting the reliability and longevity of civil steel struc-
tures. Researchers have investigated the potential of quantum computation to analyze
materials’ corrosivity [73–75]. Akrom et al. [76] evaluated the performance of a Quan-
tum Machine Learning (QML) model to predict the corrosion behavior of stainless steel.
The authors found that the Quantum Support Vector Classifier (QSVC) outperformed
classical models, achieving accuracies of 95.46% and 94.80% for Dataset A and Dataset

Furthermore, the QML technique demonstrated the ability to predict corrosion behav-

The application of QML in studying corrosion inhibition of compounds has also shown
superior performance compared with conventional methodologies [77–79]. In addition,

ment of metal quality and facilitate early and more effective corrosion detection.

1. The Finite Element Analysis (FEA) method is one of the most widely used techniques
for detailed structural investigation [85]. FEA can be significantly enhanced by

a well-known quantum algorithm capable of solving linear systems of equations [86],

In analyzing engineering systems, selecting appropriate variables to represent system
behavior is crucial. For example, forces or deflections characterize structural systems;
motions or velocities define mechanisms; and heat fluxes or temperatures describe heat
transfer problems. These are often represented through matrix formulations.
A matrix representation of a three-qubit quantum truss can be constructed from
governing equations, enabling the calculation of displacement and rotation values of
the system. Similarly, this quantum conceptualization could be extended to traffic flow
optimization, resource allocation in construction management, and mechanical system
computations, significantly improving analytical performance and efficiency.
2. Graph formalism enables eigenvalue conditions to be expressed as secular equations
for matrices of finite dimension. However, when an engineering system is not well
defined, the resulting mathematical manipulations may lead to inconsistent or
unsolvable formulations [87].
Quantum graph theory is an active research area that establishes isomorphisms between
quantum states and graph structures, providing a framework for developing quantum-
isomorphic representations applicable to civil engineering system analysis. Musto et al.
[88] demonstrated that graphs quantum-isomorphic to a given graph are in bijective
correspondence with Morita equivalence classes possessing finite-dimensional rep-

mathematical framework for modelling engineering metamaterials.
The combination of graph theory with quantum mechanics could lead to novel
approaches for analyzing vibrational states in engineering structures. This integration
also supports multidisciplinary applications, including vibration modelling of coupled
plates, quantum walks, and search algorithms [90]. For instance, quantum minimum-
finding algorithms can be adapted to solve problems such as the shortest path and graph
connectivity in rail infrastructure networks, leveraging Grover’s algorithm for enhanced
computational efficiency.

mathematics [87]. A Quantum Combinatorial Representation (QCR) can be
developed by integrating discrete linear programming, matroid theory, graph theory,
and quantum theory. Through this approach, new proofs of established theorems and
computational methods can be formulated in novel ways—now expressed within the
framework of quantum combinatorial theorems embedded in QCR.

5.1 Application aspects
Quantum Civil Engineering represents a transformative paradigm that integrates quan-
tum computing concepts, technologies, and operations into the design, construction,
and management of infrastructure systems. As quantum technologies advance, their
applications in civil engineering are expected to be vast and revolutionary [143]. The fol-
lowing highlights key areas where quantum computing could reshape the discipline:
1. Project planning and design play an indispensable role in the successful completion of
a project. The influence of quantum algorithm can change method of analysis for site
condition, environmental impact and material properties to optimize infrastructural
designs for durability, efficiency and sustainability.
2. Multiple analysis and design iterations in structural engineering require excellent
precision and efficiency. Quantum algorithms can analyse complicated structural

models, predict potential failure modes and optimize designs to be safe and compliant
with codes and specifications.
3. In building sustainability creating eco-friendly alternatives is essential. Simulating
molecular reactions at a high precision with quantum computing can design
sustainable materials to reduce the use of harmful substances. In addition, energy
consumption within buildings can be optimized with quantum algorithm. Light
designs and HVAC systems can be optimized to minimize environmental impact and
reduce operational cost.
4. Optimizing traffic flow to influence vehicle movement and reduce congestion through
modern technology requires improvement. Quantum algorithms can identify optimal
routes and minimize delay. Similarly, quantum supply chain optimization would
provide means to reduce cost of material procurement, reducing waste and enhancing
project efficiency.
5. Quantum computing can accelerate the development of new construction material
with superior properties. Simulations through quantum can model material behaviour
at molecular and atomic level, which enables innovative material design with enhanced
durability and strength.
6. Maintenance strategies can be predicted through quantum computing, which would
assist to prevent equipment failure and downtime in construction sites. By analysing
machine learning models and sensor data, quantum algorithms can optimize
performance of structures and equipment that requires early maintenance.
7. Surveying is a major part of building projects. Quantum sensors would theoretically
detect pipeline, underground obstructions and mine shaft before the progress of the
impact. Impacting this would save time and funds from unexpected obstacles.
8. Monitoring of air, water and soil pollution with real time data to guide effective
environmental protection can be achieved with quantum sensors. In addition,
Quantum dot-based sensors can detect pollutant, monitor water quality and other
crucial water parameters in real-time. Similarly, quantum computing can optimize
irrigation systems to assist farmers and urban cities to make efficient use of water.
9. With quantum computing simulations, modelling the behaviour of complex civil
structural systems at a quantum level would attain deeper insights.
10. Image processing techniques are used to process images in order to detect features
like cracks. To enhance safety, integrity and longevity of infrastructures, quantum
nano sensors can be utilized to monitor structures health.
11. Quantum data analysis and infrastructure monitoring would be a hot topic for civil
infrastructures, because data processing would be more accurate to predict complex
problems. Through quantum computation more efficient algorithm, improved
anomaly detection and predictive analysis would be achieved.
Despite these promising prospects, scaling quantum technologies to practical civil engi-
neering applications remains challenging. Achieving this will require substantial invest-
ment, interdisciplinary collaboration, and the development of new standards to guide
implementation in the coming decades.

6 Conclusions
The progress of the quantum era may seem gradual, yet, like the internet, its transforma-
tive potential is undeniable. As quantum computing evolves, its application to civil engi-
neering will enable faster processing of complex and dynamic variables.
Mapping real-world civil engineering challenges to quantum algorithms is essential for
advancing efficient and sustainable design. Although still in its early stage, integrating
quantum computing into civil engineering promises to enhance efficiency, safety, and
sustainability. This review is aimed at physicist, mathematicians, computer scientist and
civil engineers to outline state of the art techniques for civil structures. The future of the
field will not be shaped solely by steel, concrete, or soil, but also by qubits and quantum
algorithms—ushering in the era of Quantum Civil Engineering.
