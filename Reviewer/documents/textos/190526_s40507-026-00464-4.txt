Abstract
Quantum computing has rapidly emerged as one of the fastest-growing research
domains, driven by both technological breakthroughs and the promise of
computational advantages over classical systems. This study presents a
comprehensive bibliometric analysis of the global quantum computing literature
from 1980 to 2025, comprising 31,662 publications indexed in Scopus and
accumulating over 800,000 citations. Using advanced bibliometric
techniques—including co-authorship mapping, keyword co-occurrence networks,
co-citation analysis, and centrality measures—we characterize publication trends,
institutional contributions, collaborative structures, and thematic evolution across the
ﬁeld. The results reveal exponential growth in output since 2015, led primarily by the
United States, China, and Europe, with key institutions such as MIT, the Chinese
Academy of Sciences, CNRS, and the University of Waterloo at the forefront. The
intellectual structure exhibits a dual-core conﬁguration, with foundational algorithmic
work (e.g. Shor, Grover, Nielsen & Chuang) tightly connected to emerging NISQ-era
applications such as quantum machine learning, cryptography, and hybrid
algorithms. Keyword analysis reveals the progressive expansion from core quantum
algorithms toward increasingly diverse applied domains in healthcare, ﬁnance,
chemistry, energy, and climate modeling. Public funding bodies (e.g. NSFC, NSF,
European Commission, DOE, DoD, MoST) and private-sector investment from major
technology ﬁrms are jointly driving this translational acceleration. Together, these
ﬁndings provide an integrated quantitative map of the ﬁeld’s evolution, oﬀering
valuable insight for future scientiﬁc policy, international collaboration, and strategic
funding initiatives.

and technology, rooted in the exploitation of quantum mechanical phenomena such as su-

capabilities of classical systems. By operating on qubits rather than classical bits, quan-
tum computers can explore exponentially larger state spaces, oﬀering potential break-

The theoretical foundations of quantum computing were progressively established in
the late 20th century (Fig. 1). In 1973, Charles H. Bennett introduced the concept of logical
reversibility of computation, demonstrating that computation could, in principle, be per-
formed without energy dissipation if information loss was avoided [2]. This result bridged
computation with the laws of thermodynamics and initiated early discourse on the phys-
ical realizability of computation. Shortly thereafter, Yuri Manin (1980) postulated that
quantum systems could serve as computational devices beyond the capabilities of classical
machines, introducing the notion of quantum automata [3]. That same year, Paul Benioﬀ
constructed the ﬁrst quantum mechanical model of the Turing machine, formalizing the
compatibility of quantum mechanics with computational theory [4].
A major conceptual breakthrough occurred in 1982, when Richard P. Feynman argued
that the eﬃcient simulation of quantum systems is intractable for classical computers but
could be naturally implemented on quantum hardware. This insight established quantum
simulation as a primary motivation for developing quantum computers [4, 5]. Building on
these developments, David Deutsch, in 1985, proposed the formal framework of the uni-
versal quantum computer, capable of simulating any physical process governed by quan-
tum mechanics, thus generalizing the Church-Turing thesis to the quantum domain [6].
The 1990s saw the ﬁrst concrete demonstrations of quantum computational advantage
through algorithmic breakthroughs. The Deutsch–Jozsa algorithm (1992) oﬀered the ﬁrst
clear example of exponential speedup for certain oracle problems [7]. In 1993, Daniel Si-
mon introduced a problem exhibiting an exponential separation between classical and
quantum computational complexity, directly inspiring Peter Shor’s 1997 polynomial-time
quantum algorithm for integer factorization and discrete logarithms [8]. Shor’s algorithm
had profound implications for modern cryptography, undermining the mathematical as-
sumptions underpinning RSA and other widely deployed public-key encryption schemes
[9]. In parallel, Grover’s algorithm (1996) achieved quadratic speedup for unstructured
search problems, further expanding the applicability of quantum computing [10].
Concurrently, major advances were made in quantum communication. In 1993, Charles
H. Bennett and collaborators introduced the quantum teleportation protocol, demonstrat-
ing that an unknown quantum state could be faithfully transferred between distant loca-
tions through a combination of shared entanglement and classical communication [11].
This foundational result laid the groundwork for subsequent developments in quantum
communication networks, quantum repeaters, and long-distance entanglement distribu-
tion.
These theoretical advances rapidly stimulated experimental eﬀorts. By 1997, both quan-
tum key distribution (QKD) over ﬁber-optic links (extending beyond 20 km) [12], and
experimental demonstrations of quantum teleportation were achieved [13]. In the follow-
ing years, small-scale quantum processors successfully implemented simpliﬁed versions of
Grover’s and Shor’s algorithms: two-qubit systems at Berkeley (1998) [14], three-qubit im-
plementations at IBM (1999) [15], and by 2000, Los Alamos National Laboratory achieved
a seven-qubit liquid-state NMR realization of Shor’s factoring algorithm—marking one of
the ﬁrst experimental demonstrations of algorithmic scalability in quantum computing
[16, 17].

Throughout the 2000s, steady progress was made in the physical realization of scalable
quantum hardware. Ion-trap platforms, superconducting circuits, photonic architectures,
and nuclear spin systems demonstrated increasing levels of coherence, gate ﬁdelities, and
multi-qubit control [16–20]. In parallel, the algorithmic frontier also advanced. In 2008,
Harrow, Hassidim, and Lloyd introduced the HHL algorithm, providing an eﬃcient quan-
tum solution for systems of linear equations—a result that subsequently laid the ground-
work for quantum machine learning protocols [21].
Entering the 2010s, the ﬁeld experienced an unprecedented phase of industrialization
and global investment. Yale demonstrated the ﬁrst solid-state superconducting quantum
processor in 2009 [22], while D-Wave Systems introduced a 128-qubit quantum annealer
in 2011 [16, 17]. The development of hybrid quantum-classical algorithms further ex-

panded the applicability of early quantum devices. The variational quantum eigensolver
(VQE), proposed in 2014, together with amplitude ampliﬁcation protocols, enabled the
ﬁrst practical applications in quantum chemistry, ﬁnance, and optimization on noisy
intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices [23].
In 2016, IBM democratized public access to quantum hardware through its IBM Quan-
tum Experience platform, allowing global users to program and execute circuits on su-
perconducting processors via cloud access [24]. A major milestone was reached in 2019
when Google’s Sycamore processor demonstrated quantum computational supremacy by
performing a random circuit sampling task in approximately 200 seconds, compared to
an estimated 10,000 years on classical supercomputers [25].
Subsequent years have seen rapid technological advancements. In 2022, the Nobel Prize
in Physics was awarded to Alain Aspect, John F. Clauser, and Anton Zeilinger for their pio-
neering experiments on quantum entanglement and the violation of Bell inequalities [26],
providing deﬁnitive experimental conﬁrmation of the nonlocal correlations that consti-
tute a fundamental resource for quantum computation, communication, and cryptogra-
phy [17]. In 2023, IBM unveiled its Condor processor, the ﬁrst commercial gate-based
quantum device surpassing 1000 qubits (1121 qubits) [17, 18]. In 2025, Microsoft intro-
duced a prototype of a topological qubit architecture based on Majorana modes, oﬀering
intrinsic error protection [27]. Trapped-ion platforms such as Quantinuum have demon-
strated fault-tolerant operations and certiﬁed quantum randomness on 50-qubit systems
[28]. Throughout this period, companies such as IBM, Google, and Xanadu have oﬀered
cloud access to quantum systems—by early 2024, over 600,000 registered users had ex-
ecuted more than three trillion circuits and published nearly 2900 papers using Qiskit
and IBM Quantum hardware—highlighting the explosive growth of quantum computing
research [18, 29].
Despite these advances, quantum computing remains predominantly within the NISQ
era [30], where devices are limited by noise, decoherence, and operational errors, typi-
cally operating with 50–100 qubits. Within these constraints, proof-of-concept demon-
strations have been realized across multiple application domains, including molecular
modeling for drug discovery [31–34], portfolio optimization in ﬁnance [32, 35], mate-
rials discovery for energy storage and catalysis [31], and early deployments of quantum
key distribution for secure communications [35–38]. Researchers are actively exploring
hybrid quantum-classical algorithms to extend computational capabilities in areas such
as supply chain optimization, climate modeling, and logistics. The overarching objective
remains the transition to fully fault-tolerant quantum computing (FTQC), which requires
driving physical error rates below the threshold necessary for scalable error-corrected ar-
chitectures. Achieving this milestone would unlock large-scale quantum systems capable
of executing complex simulations, real-time optimizations, and industrial-grade quantum
algorithms across multiple sectors [30].
The exponential growth of the ﬁeld has recently prompted a surge in bibliometric anal-
yses (2023–2025), yet a critical review identiﬁes distinct limitations in their scope or cur-
rency. A signiﬁcant portion of this recent literature, particularly the extensive body of
work by Sood et al., concentrates on highly speciﬁc subdomains. These include analyses
restricted to sustainable energy applications [39], materials science [40], physics-informed
models [41], or quantum-inspired metaheuristics [42]. Other niche studies focus on post-
quantum cryptography [43], climate science [44], quantum education [45], quantum re-

2 Materials and methods
2.1 Data source and retrieval strategy
The bibliometric dataset was constructed through a structured search conducted in the
Scopus database. The search targeted publications related to the broad ﬁeld of Quan-
tum Computing, while systematically incorporating associated subdomains in quantum
information, communication, cryptography, sensing, metrology, hardware, simulation,
and software development to comprehensively capture the multidisciplinary landscape
of quantum technologies. The time span covered publications from 1980 through May
11, 2025. No language restrictions were applied. Eligible document types included journal
articles, reviews, book chapters, books, and conference proceedings.
To ensure high speciﬁcity and relevance, the search query was applied to the TITLE-
ABS-KEY ﬁeld using a complex Boolean strategy designed to retrieve publications di-
rectly addressing quantum computing and its technological ecosystem. A set of exclusion
ﬁlters was implemented to eliminate documents primarily focused on unrelated mate-
rials science or condensed matter physics topics where “quantum” is often used in non-
computational contexts. Speciﬁcally, documents referencing neutron scattering, polycrys-
talline materials, thin ﬁlms, boundary conditions, or chemical reaction theories were ex-
cluded.
The search expression was organized into seven thematic modules representing: (1)
quantum computing and quantum-mechanical computational models; (2) quantum in-
formation science, technology, and communication; (3) quantum cryptography and post-
quantum cryptographic approaches; (4) quantum sensing and quantum control; (5) quan-
tum metrology, tomography, clocks, gravimetry, and imaging; (6) quantum hardware sys-
tems and device architectures, and (7) quantum simulation, algorithms, and software
frameworks. These thematic modules reﬂect the conceptual structure of the search design.
The full Scopus query, including all Boolean operators, truncation rules, proximity oper-
ators, and ﬁeld speciﬁcations exactly as implemented, is provided in the Supplementary
Materials (Table S1). The complete search protocol, including scripts, search strings, and
preprocessing pipelines, is publicly archived at: https://github.com/DanielCondeTorres/
BIBLIOMETRIC.
All retrieved records were exported in CSV format, capturing full bibliographic meta-
data including author names, aﬃliations, document titles, abstracts, keywords, source ti-
tles, publication years, citation counts, and full reference lists.

2.2 Data cleaning and preprocessing
A multi-stage data cleaning process was applied to ensure dataset integrity and repro-
ducibility. Duplicate records were identiﬁed and removed using a combination of DOI
matching and title similarity algorithms. Aﬃliations and author names were standard-
ized using Pandas (v2.2.3) [56] and thefuzz (1.2.2) [https://github.com/seatgeek/thefuzz]
to handle variations (e.g., “MIT” vs. “Massachusetts Institute of Technology”). Records
lacking essential bibliographic ﬁelds or exhibiting inconsistent metadata were systemati-
cally excluded. This procedure yielded a curated and validated corpus of quantum comput-
ing research publications across multiple subﬁelds. The ﬁnal cleaned dataset consisted of
31,662 documents, exported in CSV format with comprehensive metadata including au-
thor names, aﬃliations, titles, abstracts, keywords, sources, publication years, citations,
and reference lists.

3 Results and discussion
3.1 General descriptive statistics
The bibliometric dataset comprises 31,662 quantum computing publications authored
by 66,171 unique contributors between 1980 and 2025. Of these, 3618 articles (11.4%)
are single-authored, while multi-authored collaborations dominate with 28,044 papers
(88.6%). The Collaboration Index (CI) [57]—deﬁned as the average number of authors
per paper—is 3.97, indicating that nearly four authors collaborate on each article on av-
erage. The Collaboration Coeﬃcient (CC) [57, 70], which adjusts for the presence of large
author teams to better reﬂect actual collaboration intensity, stands at 0.621. A CC value
above 0.5 indicates a strong propensity toward multi-authorship and complex collabora-
tion structures across the ﬁeld.
The distribution of team sizes follows a long-tailed pattern (Fig. 2): teams of three au-
thors are the most frequent (6887 articles; 21.8%), followed by two-author (6679; 21.1%)
and four-author (5171; 16.3%) papers. Single-author works form a small minority (3618;
11.4%), while large collaborations (≥10 authors) are less frequent but present. These pat-
terns collectively reveal that small-to-medium-sized collaborative teams dominate quan-
tum computing scholarship. A particularly notable case of large-scale collaboration in-
volves a paper authored by 288 contributors describing the construction of the CUORE
cryostat for neutrinoless double-beta decay searches, which informs the development of
cryogenic systems applicable to quantum hardware [71]. While mathematically an outlier
in the dataset, this document is analytically signiﬁcant: it signals the intersection of quan-
tum computing with “Big Science” infrastructure. It demonstrates that the development of
stable cryogenic environments for superconducting qubits is transitioning from lab-scale
experiments to massive engineering eﬀorts comparable to high-energy physics collabora-
tions. This shift in authorship patterns reﬂects the increasing complexity of the hardware
layer, where progress requires large-scale, multidisciplinary consortia rather than isolated
theoretical groups.
Language analysis shows an overwhelming dominance of English-language publica-
tions. Of the total documents, 31,149 (98.4%) are published in English. Chinese follows
with 477 documents (1.5%), while all other languages combined (Russian, Portuguese,
Japanese, Spanish, Turkish, Korean, German, etc.) represent less than 1% of the dataset,

3.2 Global scientiﬁc production and collaboration patterns
The international landscape of quantum computing research reﬂects both scientiﬁc am-
bition and national strategic priorities, with a small number of countries accounting for
the majority of global output. As shown in Fig. 5, the United States currently leads in total
publication volume, contributing 27,092 articles, followed closely by China with 25,407.
Germany ranks third (7893), while India emerges as a major contributor within the Asia-
Paciﬁc region (7305). This global distribution highlights the concentration of quantum
computing research within a limited set of scientiﬁcally advanced and heavily invested
national systems.

slightly diﬀerent pattern. China leads with 6695 articles, followed by the United States

to leading European nations. A more striking contrast is found in India, which shows the
lowest ratio of international partnership (approx. 21%). On the other hand, smaller con-
tributors like Canada and Australia feature more balanced networks, with Multi-Country
Publications (MCP) representing a much larger portion of their output compared to the

nologies. The U.S. National Science Foundation (NSF) has similarly awarded nearly 2450
grants, while the European Commission—primarily through Horizon 2020 and related
programs—has funded more than 2000 projects. Additional contributions from China’s
Ministry of Science and Technology (MoST), the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE),
and the U.S. Department of Defense (DoD) have further strengthened basic and applied
research capacities, with particular emphasis on infrastructure development, quantum
sensing, secure communication, and defense applications. Together, these six agencies—
NSFC, NSF, European Commission, MoST, DOE, and DoD—account for the vast majority
of public-sector backing in quantum-computing research, illustrating how both national
priorities and supranational collaborations have jointly propelled the ﬁeld from proof-of-
principle experiments toward early commercial deployments.
The dominance of these public funding bodies in the bibliometric metadata has pro-
found implications for the structure of the ﬁeld previously identiﬁed in Fig. 10. The high
centrality of the NSFC, NSF, and European Commission suggests that the transition from
theoretical algorithms to NISQ-era applications is not yet driven by pure market dynam-
ics, but rather by a “state-led industrialization” model. These agencies act as the primary
drivers de-risking the translation of foundational theories (the red cluster in co-citation
analysis) into experimental hardware (the blue cluster). Consequently, the bibliometric
evidence indicates that the global quantum ecosystem remains structurally dependent on
strategic public ﬁnancing to sustain the publication growth observed since 2015.
4 Conclusions
This study has presented a comprehensive bibliometric mapping of the global quantum
computing research ecosystem over the period 1980–2025. By integrating publication
trends, citation performance, collaboration structures, conceptual evolution, and trans-
lational developments, the analysis delineates the complex dynamics that have driven the
ﬁeld’s rapid expansion from theoretical foundations to emerging industrial applications.

The quantitative data conﬁrm that quantum computing has transitioned from its early
algorithmic and information-theoretic roots into a highly multidisciplinary domain en-
compassing hardware engineering, software development, cryptography, materials sci-
ence, chemistry, and ﬁnance. The exponential growth in publication output, particularly
since 2015, reﬂects both scientiﬁc maturation and large-scale public and private invest-
ment.
The structural analysis of collaboration networks reveals a tightly interconnected global
research system, with strong international partnerships coexisting alongside concentrated
national eﬀorts—particularly in the United States, China, and Europe. Co-citation and
keyword analyses conﬁrm and quantify this expected “dual-core” intellectual architecture.
Our metrics demonstrate that while the ﬁeld is divided between foundational theory and
NISQ-era applications, these domains remain structurally coupled by speciﬁc algorithmic
bridges (e.g., VQE, QAOA) rather than fragmenting into isolated silos.
Translational activities are accelerating, with quantum computing now being actively
explored across multiple industrial sectors, including healthcare, ﬁnance, energy, climate
science, materials engineering, and chemistry. These eﬀorts are supported by unprece-
dented levels of public-sector funding from major national and supranational agencies, as
well as by signiﬁcant private-sector investment from leading technology companies and
ﬁnancial markets. The emergence of ﬁnancial instruments such as quantum-focused ETFs
underscores growing investor conﬁdence in the ﬁeld’s medium- and long-term prospects.
Nonetheless, many technical challenges remain before large-scale fault-tolerant quan-
tum computing can be fully realized. Current eﬀorts continue to focus on improving hard-
ware coherence, error correction, qubit scaling, and algorithmic robustness. In parallel,
the hybridization of quantum and classical approaches oﬀers near-term opportunities for
practical advantage in selected domains.
Overall, the bibliometric evidence presented here illustrates not only the remarkable sci-
entiﬁc progress of quantum computing but also its progressive integration into broader
technological and economic ecosystems. Continued interdisciplinary collaboration, sus-
tained funding, and coordinated policy eﬀorts will be essential to drive the ﬁeld toward its
next phase of scalable, fault-tolerant, and industrially transformative quantum computing
systems.
