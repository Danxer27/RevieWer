# 1. Research Fingerprint
The paper aims to solve the lack of a centralized, structured, and continuously updateable dataset for Open Peer Review (OPR) processes, particularly for High-Energy Physics (HEP). The proposed approach is the Open Review-Based (ORB) dataset, which integrates data from OpenReview.net and SciPost.org using a structured data architecture built on predefined `OrbRaw*` interfaces and target `Orb*` dataclasses, managed by an ETL pipeline. ORB distinguishes itself by providing not just the data, but also the entire infrastructure (interfaces, ETL processes, and an ontology) necessary for continuous data integration (CEDIgaR). For the central claim—that ORB is a valuable, extensible resource for advanced NLP research—to hold, the authors must demonstrate that the data structure and methodology are robust enough to handle the inherent heterogeneity and semantic ambiguity of scientific review text, and that the preliminary NLP experiments are not merely proof-of-concept demonstrations but reflect the true potential of the structured data.

# 2. Targeted Findings

## Finding 1: Overstated novelty of the dataset
- **Section:** Abstract
- **Excerpt:** > "In this paper, we introduce the new comprehensive Open Review-Based dataset (ORB); it includes a curated list of more than 36,000 scientific papers with their more than 89,000 reviews and final decisions. We gather this information from two sources: the OpenReview.net and SciPost.org websites."
- **Problem:** The authors claim ORB is "the new comprehensive" dataset, but the Related Work section details several existing, large-scale, and specialized OPR datasets (e.g., PeerRead, MOPRD, MReD, and the "Peer Review Workbench" from Elsevier). The claim of being the *first* or *most comprehensive* is unsubstantiated, especially since the sources are themselves derived from existing, published platforms. The novelty lies in the *infrastructure* (ETL, interfaces), not the data collection itself.
- **Severity:** Major
- **Fix:** Rephrase the claim to focus on the *integration* and *architecture* (e.g., "ORB provides a novel, unified framework for integrating and managing heterogeneous OPR data from multiple sources...") rather than the sheer size or comprehensiveness of the data.

- **Excerpt:** > "If the initial motivation for ORB is the rather specific RADNEXT project4, it is quite clear that such a resource will also be of significant value for researchers interested into assessing the impact of the paradigm-shifting OPR movement, both on the academic milieu and the society at large."
- **Problem:** The paper repeatedly frames the ORB dataset as being valuable for general OPR research, but the primary motivation is the "rather specific RADNEXT project" in High-Energy Physics (HEP). The authors fail to provide a clear, technical justification for why the structure or content of the ORB dataset (which is heavily weighted toward Computer Science conferences like NeurIPS and ICLR) is uniquely suited for HEP proposals. This creates a disconnect between the stated general value and the specific, unaddressed domain requirement.
- **Severity:** Major
- **Fix:** Dedicate a subsection in the Introduction or Related Work to explicitly discuss the unique data challenges and required features of HEP proposal reviews (e.g., specific physics terminology, experimental constraints, or unique scoring metrics) and how ORB's current structure addresses them, rather than just stating that it *will* be useful.

## Finding 3: Unjustified scope of the preliminary NLP experiments
- **Section:** 4.2 Preliminary illustrative experiments
- **Excerpt:** > "In this simplified proof-of-concept experiment, the accuracy score obtained by SVC is 0.59, while the one for kNN is 0.55. This somewhat poor result may be due to one inherent feature of SciPost data: versioning. There, the corrected text between successive versions may not change too much, so, for very similar embeddings, we are enforcing the model to learn two different decisions, which negatively affects the learning process."
- **Problem:** The authors present low-performing NLP results (accuracy $\approx 0.55-0.59$) and attribute the failure primarily to "versioning" and "very similar embeddings." This is a weak explanation for poor model performance. The authors fail to perform any ablation study or sensitivity analysis to quantify the impact of versioning or embedding similarity on the model's decision boundary. Instead, they simply state the limitation, which undermines the perceived value of the entire dataset structure.
- **Severity:** Major
- **Fix:** Either remove the quantitative results (Tables 2 and 3) and focus only on the *potential* for analysis, or, if keeping the results, perform a rigorous analysis (e.g., clustering the embeddings and testing model performance on clusters of highly similar documents) to quantify the effect of versioning, rather than just stating it.

# 4. Methodology Interrogation
- **Research design justified?** The design is justified by the need to overcome the limitations of existing datasets, as stated in Section 2: "All three datasets are strongly pre-processed by text segmentation, exclusions of parts of the submissions and data annotations. The annotation is done with external tools or by human experts; it is not an inherent feature derived from the source data." The authors argue that their proposed methodology of providing both the original source data and an interface to perform pre-processing on demand is superior to the current "strongly pre-processed" state.
- **Dataset adequate for the claims?** The dataset is large (36,949 submissions, 92,879 reviews), which is a strength. However, the dataset is critically limited in scope: "The total set of 4,917 SciPost elements... was split in two train and test subsets, with a 0.8:0.2 ratio" (Section 4.2). Furthermore, the dataset is heavily biased toward Computer Science (OpenReview.net) and general natural sciences (SciPost.org), making the claim of being a general resource for "High-Energy Physics" (HEP) questionable without more domain-specific data.
- **Evaluation metrics appropriate?** For the acceptance prediction, the authors use accuracy (0.59 for SVC, 0.55 for kNN). This metric is inappropriate because the dataset is highly imbalanced (e.g., 977 accepted vs. 7 rejected in Table 2). The authors should report and discuss metrics like Precision, Recall, F1-score, and Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC) to provide a fair assessment of the model's performance on the minority class (rejection).
- **Ablations or controls missing?** The authors test the combined model (SVC/kNN) but fail to isolate the contribution of the textual embeddings versus the structured metadata (e.g., the number of reviews, the final score). For instance, they claim the model predicts acceptance based on "textual embeddings and the grades with given confidence," but they never test a model that uses *only* the structured metadata (e.g., number of reviews, average score) to determine acceptance, making it impossible to attribute the predictive power to the text alone.
- **Statistical validity?** The authors report the Mean Absolute Error (MAE) for the grading prediction (Section 4.2), stating the error is "around 0.8 per final score and 0.4 per confidence." However, they provide no confidence intervals or standard deviations for these error estimates. Without this, the reported MAE values are merely point estimates and do not convey the statistical reliability of the prediction.
