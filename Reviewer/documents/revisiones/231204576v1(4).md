# 1. Research Fingerprint
The paper aims to solve the lack of a centralized, structured, and continuously updateable dataset for Open Peer Review (OPR) processes, particularly for High-Energy Physics (HEP). The proposed approach is the Open Review-Based (ORB) dataset, which integrates data from OpenReview.net and SciPost.org using a structured data architecture built on predefined `OrbRaw*` interfaces and target `Orb*` dataclasses, managed by an ETL pipeline. ORB distinguishes itself by providing not just the data, but also the entire infrastructure (interfaces, ETL processes, and an ontology) necessary for continuous data integration (CEDIgaR). For the central claim—that ORB is a valuable, extensible resource for advanced NLP research—to hold, the authors must demonstrate that the data structure and methodology (CEDIgaR) are robust enough to handle the inherent heterogeneity and semantic ambiguity of scientific review text across vastly different scientific domains, and that the preliminary NLP experiments are not merely proof-of-concept demonstrations but reflect the true potential of the structured data.

# 2. Targeted Findings

## Finding 1: Overstated Novelty of the Dataset
- **Section:** Abstract
- **Excerpt:** > "In this paper, we introduce the new comprehensive Open Review-Based dataset (ORB); it includes a curated list of more than 36,000 scientific papers with their more than 89,000 reviews and final decisions. We gather this information from two sources: the OpenReview.net and SciPost.org websites."
- **Problem:** The claim of introducing a "new comprehensive" dataset is misleading because the authors explicitly state that the data is gathered from two existing, publicly accessible platforms (OpenReview.net and SciPost.org). The novelty lies in the *infrastructure* (the ETL, interfaces, and CEDIgaR methodology), not the raw data itself. This overstatement diminishes the perceived contribution of the data collection effort.
- **Severity:** Major
- **Fix:** Rephrase the abstract to emphasize that ORB is a *curated, structured, and extensible framework* built upon existing public data sources, rather than presenting the data collection as the primary novelty.

## Finding 2: Insufficient Justification for Data Extraction Methodology
- **Section:** 3.1 SciPost.org
- **Excerpt:** > "However, the major disadvantage, in comparison to OpenReview.net, is the lack of any API allowing data access. All the data is available only as a website; in order to obtain relevant information, the data needs to be extracted from every single HTML page and represented as a data object."
- **Problem:** The authors state that they implemented an "own scrapper" for SciPost.org, but they provide no technical details, pseudocode, or discussion of the scraping challenges (e.g., handling dynamic content, anti-bot measures, or structural changes in the HTML). This lack of methodological transparency makes the data collection process non-reproducible and weakens the claim of providing a robust, reusable ETL process.
- **Severity:** Critical
- **Fix:** Provide a detailed technical description of the scraping process for SciPost.org, including the libraries used (e.g., BeautifulSoup, Selenium), how dynamic content was handled, and how the scraper was robustly designed to mitigate site changes.

## Finding 3: Unjustified Scope of NLP Experiments

- **Excerpt:** > "Based on the embeddings inferred from the above described model, we could predict the acceptance of the submitted papers present in the ORB dataset. - **Problem:** The authors use the SciPost subset for the acceptance prediction experiment but then use a different, larger subset (over 16,000 submissions) for the grading statistics prediction, which includes "conferences such as NeurIPS and ICLR but also UAI." - **Severity:** Major

# 4. Methodology Interrogation
- **Research design justified?** The design is justified by the need to overcome the limitations of existing datasets, as stated in Section 2: "All the data sources presented often gather abstracts and decisions, or abstracts and comments, or reviews only, while our main goal is to collect the data for multi-purpose experiments with special attention towards automatic assessment and, possibly, to use it in the field of High-Energy-Physics experiments."
- **Dataset adequate for the claims?** The dataset size (36,949 submissions, 92,879 reviews) is large, but the adequacy is questionable because the data is highly heterogeneous and incomplete. The authors note that "All three datasets are strongly pre-processed by text segmentation, exclusions of parts of the submissions and data annotations. The annotation is done with external tools or by human experts; it is not an inherent feature derived from the source data." This suggests the data is heavily filtered and may not represent the full, natural scope of scientific discourse required for robust NLP training.
- **Evaluation metrics appropriate?** For the acceptance prediction, the authors use "accuracy score" (0.59 for SVC, 0.55 for kNN). While accuracy is a standard metric, it is insufficient for imbalanced classification tasks (which acceptance prediction often is). Furthermore, the authors only report the confusion matrix and accuracy, but do not report precision, recall, or F1-score, which are necessary to fully characterize the model's performance, especially given the stated difficulty with versioning.
- **Ablations or controls missing?** The authors test two classifiers (SVC and kNN) but fail to isolate the contribution of the embedding model itself. They train the Doc2Vec model on a general corpus ("scientific\_papers dataset [Cohan et al., 2018]") and then apply it to ORB. They do not test if the embeddings learned *specifically* from the ORB corpus (or a combination of ORB and the general corpus) would yield better results, making it impossible to attribute the observed performance to the ORB data structure versus the general corpus training.
- **Statistical validity?** The grading statistics prediction uses MAE (Mean Absolute Error) and reports values (0.87, 0.40, etc.). However, the authors do not provide the standard deviation or confidence intervals for these error estimates. Reporting only a single MAE value for a complex prediction task is statistically weak, as it does not convey the reliability or variability of the model's prediction error.
