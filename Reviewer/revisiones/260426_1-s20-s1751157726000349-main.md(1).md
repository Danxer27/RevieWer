# Critical Review of "Relationship between peer review quality and scientific impact: Insights from LLMs-assessed reviews"

## 1. **Summary of the Paper**  
The manuscript investigates how automated evaluation of peer reviews (using Google’s Gemini 2.0 Flash) correlates with post-publication citations, positing that higher-quality reviews enhance citation counts. The authors claim a ~5-citation increase per review point.

## 2. **Novelty and Significance**  
- **Strengths**: Pioneers automated peer review assessment using the RQI tool (common in biomedical fields) on eLife’s open PRRs—a novel dataset for this domain.  
- **Limitations**: Novel only within life sciences/biomedicine; generalizability to other disciplines is untested.

## 3. **Strengths**  
- Demonstrated high inter-rater reliability (κ ~0.99) between human and LLM evaluations.  
- Robustness checks with log citations, top-citation exclusion, and negative binomial models confirm results.  
- Tested zero-shot prompts as a baseline control.

## 4. **Weaknesses**  
### Methodological Concerns:  
1. **Domain Bias in RQI**: The tool was validated for biomedical papers but lacks justification for application to other fields (e.g., social sciences). Results may not generalize.  
2. **Prompt Engineering Limitations**: Few-shot and zero-shot prompts were used; no fine-tuning or bias mitigation steps described, risking model overfitting to eLife’s PRR style.  
3. **Causality vs. Correlation**: Cites as impact proxies are influenced by novelty/popularity, not solely review quality. Uncontrolled confounders (e.g., journal prestige) threaten validity.  
4. **Coefficient Interpretation**: A 5k citation increase per point is meaningful in low-citation fields but may overstate effects if citations plateau or inflate across journals.

### Reproducibility Issues:  
- No details on Gemini 2.0 Flash’s configuration (e.g., temperature, training data).  
- PRRs dataset access restricted; authors should cite DOIs and verify sample representativeness.

## 5. **Methodology & Experimental Rigor**  
- **Flaws**: Assumed domain invariance of RQI without external validation. Control variables (title length) may reflect readability rather than quality.  
- **Statistical Oversights**: Small effect sizes in high-citation fields could mask true impacts; normality assumptions not verified.

## 6. **Reproducibility Checklist**  
- **Missing Details**: Exact prompt templates, temperature settings, and model hyperparameters are undisclosed.  
- **Dataset Access**: PRRs from eLife require institutional login—prevents independent verification.

## 7. **Required Improvements Before Submission**  
1. Validate RQI across non-biomedical domains (e.g., via cross-domain experiments).  
2. Document Gemini 2.0 Flash’s training/inference settings rigorously.  
3. Address potential confounders (novelty, journal impact) in regression models.  
4. Explore bias mitigation techniques for LLMs and compare with diverse human evaluators.  
5. Test RQI on non-open-review datasets to confirm generalizability.

## 8. **Final Verdict**  
**Major Revisions Needed**  

### Justification:  
- The core innovation (automated PRR assessment) is undermined by domain-specific tool application and insufficient bias checks.  
- Results lack external validity beyond biomedical fields, risking rejection for methodological narrowness. Authors must address these gaps to advance the field.
