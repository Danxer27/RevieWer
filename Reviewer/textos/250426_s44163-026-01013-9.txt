intelligence vs artificial peer review intelligence: 
perspectives on future disruptive sciences
Nilesh Kumar Sharma1,4*

1  Introduction
In broader perspectives, human intelligence is attributed to the general mental ability for 
reasoning, problem-solving, learning, self-awareness, and integration of cognitive func­
tions such as perception, emotions, attention, memory, language, planning, comprehen­
sion, critical thinking, creativity, abstraction, logic, and understanding of facts and data 
[1–3].
Discover Artificial Intelligence
Abstract
Human-assisted Traditional Peer Review Intelligence (HATPRINT) plays a crucial role 
in advancing scientific discoveries through critical evaluation and expert insights by 
humans. However, the rise of Artificial Intelligence-assisted Peer Review Intelligence 
(AIPRINT), powered by Generative AI (Gen AI), including various forms of LLMs is 
likely to transform the peer review landscape. While Gen AI can enhance efficiency, 
automate reviews, and refine research, overreliance may risk losing innovation and 
perpetuate pseudo-peer review cycles. Several AI-assisted review systems, such as 
SciScore, Paperpal, and various other publishers in-house AI reviewer suggestion 
tools, suggest futurist dominance of AIPRINT over HATPRINT. These platforms could 
enhance, rather than replace, human evaluation by automating checks, improving 
writing, and aiding reviewer selection. Theoretically, their integration could lead to 
the growing convergence of HATPRINT and AIPRINT leading to a semi-autonomous 
peer review systems strongly underscoring the need for governance, transparency, 
and human oversight. We propose a balanced convergence of HATPRINT and 
AIPRINT to maintain scientific integrity, ensuring human expertise remains integral. 
However, explicit limitations are lack for empirical analysis in support of the proposed 
viewpoints. Future research should explore this equilibrium to sustain disruptive 
science while leveraging AI for enhanced accuracy, productivity, and the evolution of 
peer review.

Among the various forms of human intelligence, the scientific peer review process 
embodies key attributes such as comprehension, critical thinking, creativity, abstraction, 
logic, and the interpretation of facts and data [2–5]. Human-assisted traditional peer 
review intelligence (HATPRINT) represents human-centered evaluative intelligence 
characterized by expert judgment, contextual understanding, analytical reasoning, cre­
ativity, and ethical discernment applied during assessment of research manuscripts and 
innovations.
In the past, HATPRINT has ensured rigor, accuracy, and credibility in scientific pub­
lishing, shaping the evolution of disruptive science. Beyond publication review, it also 
underpins the evaluation of research projects, their outcomes (such as papers and pat­
ents), and the identification of gaps that can guide future investigations toward more 
innovative discoveries.
It is important to discuss how Generative Artificial Intelligence (Gen AI), including 
Large Language Models (LLMs) such as OpenAI’s GPT series (ChatGPT), Google’s 
Gemini, Anthropic’s Claude, and Meta’s Llama, alongside powerful open-source options 
like Mixtral, Mistral, DeepSeek, and Falcon etc. will shape HATPRINT and its role in 
driving disruptive scientific discoveries. Understanding both the benefits and challenges 
of Gen AI is crucial for achieving better outcomes by HATPRINT in advancing disrup­
tive science and building a sustainable society. The use of Gen AI tools might be helpful 
for the peers/reviewers to channel their thought processes to come up with productive 
comments [5–10]. Besides these notable uses, Gen AI and LLMs are reported multiple 
functionalities such as fake-new judgment and fact-check [4, 5].
It is important to examine how the evolution of Gen AI and LLMs may play a crucial 
role on behalf of Peers/reviewers (Humans), leading to a form of Artificial Peer Review 
Intelligence (AIPRINT) [10–15]. Gen AI and LLMs will probably evolve to play the role 
of Artificial Peer Reviewer. Then, it would be rational to explore the implications of 
AIPRINT in the future path of disruptive sciences and innovations [15–20]. AIPRINT 
can be denoted as Gen AI and LLMs powered peer review intelligence to review sci­
entific documents such as manuscripts, articles, research projects, research progress 
reports, research outputs, and innovations by comprehension, abstraction, logic, think­
ing, and understanding of facts and data.
However, with the increasing potential of AIPRINT, potential risks of bias, superficial 
analysis, and ethical challenges could emerge in the long run if reliance is exclusively 
on AIPRINT. A probability of emerging a form of peer review intelligence as Pseudo 
Peer Review Intelligence (PSEUDOPRINT) due to the continuous vicious cycle of 
HATPRINT-APRINT.
In the future, the convergence of HATPRINT and AIPRINT needs to be studied and 
evaluated carefully, as it could lead to a potential issue, such as the escape velocity phe­
nomenon of peer review processes. Convergence of human machine is proposed to refer 
to the integration of HATPRINT with AIPRINT to create a balanced and collaborative 
model. In this state, LLMs manage routine and large-scale analysis, while humans con­
tribute creativity, ethical judgment, and contextual expertise, together strengthening the 
efficiency and integrity of peer review. In the last stage, the escape velocity phenome­
non is suggested to describe the tipping point at which AIPRINT surpasses HATPRINT, 
leading to primarily AI-driven peer review processes. At this stage, peer review risks 
becoming machine-dominated, raising questions about the role of human expertise,

2  Relevance of disruptive peer review intelligence (HATPRINT)
HATPRINT functions as a feedback mechanism that refines methodologies, strengthens 
arguments, and supports the emergence of disruptive discoveries. HATPRINT contrib­
utes through contextual critique and ethical oversight. By offering diverse perspectives 
and mentorship, experienced reviewers help advance scientific progress and guide the 
next generation of researchers [15–20]. Besides above role in the disruptive discover­
ies and sciences, the role of HATPRINT in ethical, social, cultural and diversity-based 
judgement beyond technical and process driven validation is crucial.
2.1  Artificial peer review intelligence and its impact
AIPRINT can be referred to as an emerging paradigm of peer review intelligence, mainly 
driven by Gen AI-based LLMs. Unlike human-led HATPRINT, AIPRINT operates by 
processing vast datasets, applying algorithmic logic, and automating review processes

3  Pseudo peer review intelligence
Pseudo Peer Review Intelligence (PSEUDOPRINT) can be described as a pseudo-form 
of peer review in which AI-generated insights may potentially dominate but are pre­
sented as human evaluations. This hybrid model blurs the boundary between HAT­
PRINT and AI-derived assessments, thereby undermining transparency, credibility, and 
accountability in scholarly and scientific evaluations. In this form, the peer review pro­
cess is formally attributed to human experts, yet the substantive intellectual contribution 
is largely influenced or even driven by Gen AI tools and large language models. Although 
PSEUDOPRINT may appear to preserve the features of HATPRINT, it effectively shifts 
the evaluative process toward AIPRINT. This shift in the convergence of HATPRINT 
and AIRPRINT could influence accountability, impacting innovation, and introducing 
ethical challenges [18–25].
4  The convergence cycle between HATPRINT, AIPRINT and PSEUDOPRINT
The convergence of HATPRINT and AIPRINT refers to the progressive integration of 
human expertise with artificial intelligence–driven peer review processes. Rather than 
existing in isolation, both systems increasingly interact to complement one another: 
HATPRINT contributes creativity, contextual knowledge, ethical judgment, and 
nuanced critique, while AIPRINT adds speed, scalability, automation, and the ability to 
process vast amounts of literature. This convergence is envisioned as a symbiotic col­
laboration in which humans and machines collectively shape a more efficient, accurate, 
and sustainable peer review ecosystem. In this model, AIPRINT handles routine and

Disruptive researchers, authors, innovators, and scholars commonly receive sugges­
tions, insights, creative viewpoints, interpretations, analysis, and other forms of human 
intelligence attributes that contribute to shaping the present and future path of dis­

Intelligence assistance to disruptive peer intelligence, suggestions, insights, creative 
viewpoints, interpretations, analysis, and other forms of inputs will be included in the 
revised draft of disruptive researchers, authors, innovators, and scholars [15–20]. The 
convergence of human-assisted disruptive peer review intelligence (HATPRINT) and 
artificial peer review intelligence (AIPRINT) is a plausible scenario. This convergence 
could lead to enhanced, productive, and processed forms of peer review intelligence 
(Fig. 1).

A vicious cycle of HATPRINT-AIPRINT could result in increased efficiency, auto­
mation, and human assistance to streamline the peer review process, improve accu­
racy, enhance the detection of errors and inconsistencies, and enhance productivity. 
The escape velocity phenomenon in the context of the convergence of HATPRINT and 
AIPRINT can refer to the peer review intelligence point at which AIPRINT surpasses 
HATPRINT to achieve revolutionized forms of peer review processes. At this point, the 
HATPRINT role by humans has to be adaptive, collaborative, and symbiotic for a sus­
tainable future world.
The “escape velocity” phenomenon in peer review intelligence refers to the criti­
cal threshold at which AIPRINT evolving into forms such as PSEUDOPRINT, surpass 
HATPRINT in influence and dominance. At this stage, the peer review process begins 
to operate autonomously, with minimal human intervention, creating a self-sustaining 
cycle of AI-based evaluation. In the cyclic continuum of peer review, “escape velocity” 
thus represents the transition point where the system moves from human-assisted mod­
els (HATPRINT–AIPRINT cycles) toward predominantly AI-governed review mecha­
nisms. The cyclic dynamics among HATPRINT, AIPRINT, and PSEUDOPRINT are 
depicted in Fig. 2, which conceptualizes the escape velocity phenomenon. At this stage, 
AI systems are capable of independently reviewing, refining, and even influencing sci­
entific content, potentially reducing human roles to oversight or validation. While this 
phenomenon may lead to rapid scalability and efficiency, it also raises concerns about 
over-reliance on AI, loss of human creativity and contextual depth, and ethical account­
ability. In essence, the escape velocity phenomenon marks the tipping point where the 
peer review ecosystem risks shifting from a collaborative model to an AI-dominated one.
Several AI-assisted review tools and prototypes are already in place that support the 
early evolution of AIPRINT within current scholarly ecosystems. For instance, SciScore 
automates screening with regard to the reproducibility, transparency, and methodolog­
ical aspects of research papers [26]. While Paperpal assists reviewers and authors for 
assistance in language help for scientific writing, readability, and logical flow through 
AI-powered contextual feedback [27]. Various AI-assisted plagiarism tools, such as Tur­
nitin [28], Qillbot [29], Grammarly [30] assist in plagiarism check and provide suitable 
feedback to enhance the quality of texts. Furthermore, AI-assisted tools for creating and 
enhancing the publication images, such as Illustrae [31] and BioRender [32] are helpful 
for the researchers to compile and present their data. Similarly, Elsevier’s reviewer sug­
gestion system employs machine learning to match manuscripts with suitable reviewers, 
and Springer Nature, IEEE, and Frontiers are experimenting with AI screening tools to 
identify statistical inconsistencies and plagiarism. These examples represent the initial 
stages of AIPRINT, where automation enhances, but does not replace, human evaluative

5  Challenges and ethical concerns with artificial peer review intelligence 
(AIPRINT)

bias, and errors to encompass comprehensive issues of accountability, authorship, and 
trust in disruptive sciences. The emergence of PSEUDOPRINT could lead to a lack of 
authenticity of scholarly evaluation if AI contributions are misrepresented as human 
judgment, and also pinpoint ethical concerns on levels and involved stakeholders. With 
regard to AIPRINT, there should be procedures to safeguard against systemic biases that 
may disproportionately privilege certain disciplines or geographies. Furthermore, ethi­
cal stewardship will require a dynamic equilibrium where human creativity, contextual

6  AI governance in peer review
Responsible AI governance in peer review requires balancing efficiency and timeliness 
with the preservation of scientific integrity and innovation. The rise of AIPRINT and its 
convergence with HATPRINT offer benefits such as scalability, improved error detec­
tion, and reduced workload for reviewers. However, risks including algorithmic bias, 
superficial assessment, and PSEUDOPRINT highlight the need for strong governance 
frameworks.
Unchecked use of AI in scholarly review may amplify systemic bias, reduce account­
ability, and obscure transparency, especially if AI-generated insights are misrepresented 
as human judgments. Governance structures must therefore ensure transparency 
through mandatory disclosure of AI involvement, human oversight, and regular audit­
ing of algorithmic decisions. To prevent the “escape velocity phenomenon,” where AI 
surpasses human review in influence and erodes contextual depth, governance systems 
should define limits and monitor transitions. Institutions, research bodies, and policy 
agencies must work toward a symbiotic model integrating HATPRINT and AIPRINT, 
guided by ethical safeguards, accountability, and transparency to foster credible and dis­
ruptive scientific innovation.
A multi-tier governance framework is essential to guide the responsible use of AI and 
peer review intelligence across preclinical and clinical research domains. At the institu­
tional level, Research Ethics Committees and Institutional Review Boards should inte­
grate AI-audit and disclosure modules to evaluate the extent of AI assistance in data 
analysis, interpretation, and peer review. Governmental agencies, such as the Depart­
ment of Science and Technology, the Indian Council of Medical Research, and the U.S. 
National Institutes of Health can establish policy directives mandating transparency, 
algorithmic explainability, and human oversight for AI-assisted reviews of preclinical 
and clinical studies. Internationally, organizations like the WHO, OECD, UNESCO, 
and the International Committee of Medical Journal Editors are positioned to harmo­
nize global standards for AI governance, ensuring ethical consistency and interopera­
bility of AI-based evaluation systems. International Research Societies and Committees

7  Conclusion
In conclusion, HATPRINT remains indispensable for sustaining scientific advance­
ment. While Gen AI will increasingly influence peer review, it is unlikely to fully replace 
human expertise. Instead, the integration of AI-driven analysis can complement human 
evaluation by enhancing objectivity, detecting biases, and improving the efficiency of 
complex, data-intensive reviews.
As disruptive sciences continue to evolve, ethical principles such as transparency, 
conflict of interest management, and bias mitigation will become central to maintain­
ing integrity within the converging landscape of HATPRINT and AIPRINT. The future 
of peer review is expected to adopt a collaborative human–AI model, that can leverage 
analytical precision alongside human contextual judgment and creativity.
Long-term studies will be needed to assess the equilibrium between HATPRINT and 
AIPRINT and to quantify their relative contributions within published research. Clear 
disclosure of AI involvement in peer review will guide researchers, innovators, and poli­
cymakers in interpreting the authenticity and credibility of scientific outputs.
In future, robust AI governance frameworks will be essential to create a sustainable 
and ethically sound peer review ecosystem one that balances productivity, transparency, 
and innovation while preventing the dominance of algorithmic evaluation.
The proposed terms HATPRINT, AIPRINT, and PSEUDOPRINT are original concep­
tualizations introduced to better articulate this evolving and convergent trajectory of 
peer review intelligence in scholarly and scientific communication.
