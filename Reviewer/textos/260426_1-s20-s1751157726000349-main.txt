A B S T R A C T
The significant rise in scientific publications has made pre-publication peer review crucial for 
safeguarding research quality and enhancing scientific impact. Yet, correlation between peer 
review quality, indicative of a rigorous peer review procedure, and the subsequent scientific 
impact is rarely investigated. Here, this study leverages Google’s large language model (LLM) 
named Gemini 2.0 Flash along with the few-shot prompt to automatically evaluate peer review 
quality, and investigate the correlation between the automatically assessed peer review quality 
(indicated by review scores) and scientific impact (measured by post-publication citations). First, 
the subsample reliability analysis reveals excellent agreement between review scores evaluated 
by humans and those generated by Gemini 2.0 Flash, underscoring the potential of LLMs in 
automatically assessing peer review quality. Then, empirical analysis indicates a positive asso­
ciation between automatically assessed review scores and citations. Specifically, a one-point in­
crease in the review score is associated with an average increase of approximately five citations 
(coefficient = 5.440, p < 0.01). This positive effect remains robust in several robustness analyses, 
including transforming citation counts into logarithmic form, excluding papers within the top 1% 
of citations, and applying a negative binomial regression model. In addition, all analyses remain 
robust when using the average and “year-normalised” citations as dependent variables, when 
using first-author information to proxy control variables, and when employing a zero-shot prompt 
for Gemini 2.0 Flash responses, despite the relatively lower estimated effect. These findings 
highlight the importance of peer review quality in enhancing the scientific impact and the po­
tential of using LLMs to automatically assess the review quality. Despite the promise, the wide­
spread adoption of LLMs in automatically assessing peer review relevant tasks warrants further 
investigation.
1. Introduction
Peer review has long been regarded as a rigorous scientific stage for research evaluation (Bornmann, 2011). By offering detailed 
and critical feedbacks from experienced researchers in addressing several aspects of a manuscript (such as the methodological rigor, 
result interpretations, and presentation), quality of the manuscript can be significantly enhanced (Mulligan et al., 2013). This, in turn, 
may influences the post-publication scientific impact (Abramo et al., 2024; Kang et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2024; Tennant & 
Ross-Hellauer, 2020). However, the exponential growth of publications has also led to a proliferation of low quality studies, often 
attributed to the failure of peer review (Bornmann & Mutz, 2015; Noorden, 2025; Van Noorden, 2023). In this context, assessing the

quality of peer reviews becomes imperative. As a higher review quality score is typically interpreted as evidence that a manuscript has 
undergone rigorous evaluation, thereby improving the overall quality and contributing to a higher scientific impact. Despite this, 
existing studies measure review quality often rely on self-assigned scores from reviewers, which is subject to individual bias and may 
lead to biased estimations (Abramo et al., 2024; Patterson & Harris, 2009). As a result, examining the relationship between peer review 
quality and scientific impact still warrants in-depth exploration.
Distinguishing from the self-evaluated process of peer review quality scores (Wu et al., 2024), the review quality instrument (RQI) 
has been well-developed to provide a more objective, accountable, and transparent assessment, particularly for peer review in life 
sciences and biomedical domain. Those instruments are used to evaluate whether reviewers effectively fulfill their responsibilities, 
including critically evaluating submissions and providing constructive feedback on several items to enhance research quality. Previous 
studies have employed RQIs to score the review quality (Van Rooyen et al., 1999); however, these efforts are often limited by small 
sample sizes due to the time-intensive nature of manual evaluation. Additionally, human assessments are susceptible to fatigue, 
subjectivity, and bias, which can compromise the reliability of the results. The recent emergence of generative AI, particularly the large 
language models (LLMs), which demonstrate proficiency across a wide range of tasks and offer a promising solution to these challenges 
(Sun, 2025).
LLMs have demonstrated significant capabilities in performing various research tasks, offering transformative potential for the 
traditional scientific research paradigm (Grossmann et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2022). Recent studies have shown that LLMs, such as 
OpenAI’s ChatGPT-4, can assist in pre-peer review processes by functioning as review checklist assistants (Goldberg et al., 2024). They 
have also shown promise in generating peer review comments that address key aspects of peer review process, including recognizing 
the strengths (Drori & Te’eni, 2024), weaknesses (Widhawati et al., 2024), clarity (Liang et al., 2024), novelty (Kadi & Aslaner, 2024; 
Tyser et al., 2024), and reproducibility (Srivastava, 2023) of a submitted paper. These capabilities have been demonstrated across 
multiple LLM models, including LLaMA2, PaLM2, among others. Moreover, LLMs have been shown to provide helpfulness ratings, 
generate recommendation scores, and even produce final decisions for submitted papers (Robertson, 2023; Saad et al., 2024; Mike 
Thelwall & Yaghi, 2024). Continuous advancements have improved the ability of LLMs to generate feedback that closely aligns with 
human reviews, offering more specific and constructive feedback on scientific papers (D’Arcy et al., 2024). These improvements 
highlight the potential of LLMs to streamline and enhance peer review tasks, ultimately contributing to the automatic peer review 
process (Thelwall, 2024b).
Further evidence suggests that LLMs can provide feedback on peer reviews that is comparable to human evaluators (Liang et al., 
2024). However, the research utilizing ChatGPT-4 have not fully integrated standardized RQIs, which are essential for evaluating key 
aspects of peer review quality, even though some aspects have been considered. Moreover, several leading LLMs, including ChatGPT-4, 
Claude (version 3.5 Sonnet), and Gemini (versions 1 and 1.5), have exhibited signs of declining cognitive performance, as their earlier 
versions outperformed more recent counterparts (Dayan et al., 2024). These concerns, coupled with the rapid pace of LLM version 
development, underscore the need for newer models with enhanced contextual understanding and processing capabilities. Google’s 
latest LLM versions have demonstrated superior performance (Thelwall, 2024a), consistently ranking first on the Hugging Face 
Chatbot Arena Leaderboard1. This positions them as strong candidates for the automated evaluation of peer review quality.
In this study, I employ Google’s most advanced LLM, Gemini 2.0 Flash, to automate the evaluation of peer review quality using the 
most widely recognized RQI tool. By incorporating those tools, this study aims to enhance the rigor of the evaluation process. Given 
that RQIs are predominantly applied in the biomedical research domain, my analysis focuses on this area, utilizing open peer review 
comments retrieved from eLife, a leading journal in the biomedical field. Specifically, the relationship between peer review quality, as 
automatically assessed by Gemini 2.0 Flash, and scientific impact, measured through post-publication citations is investigated. The 
objectives of this study are to explore: 
1). The reliability of LLM as a tool for assessing peer review quality;
2). The correlation between LLM-measured peer review quality and the impact of publications.
This study makes contribution in advancing the understanding of how the peer review quality influences scientific impact of ac­
ademic research and demonstrates the potential application of LLMs in enhancing the assessment of review quality.
2. Related work
2.1. Reviewers’ role
Serving as gatekeepers, peer reviewers hold considerable influence over the peer review process (Kang et al., 2025). The extent to 
which they maintain objectivity to ensure an unbiased peer review is still the focus that attract wide scientific attention (Lee et al., 
2013). One of such research domains utilized rich dataset attributable to the open peer review of investigating the reviewers’ role (Sun 
et al., 2022). A key focus of the extant research is to assess reviewer performance through analysis of the review scores they assigned, 
treating these ratings as indicators of their perceived research “quality” and explore the relationship between those scores and the 
subsequent scientific impact (Bornmann, 2017; Kousha & Thelwall, 2024).

For example, by using the data from Italian research assessment exercise (RAE), Abramo et al. (2024) found that papers with the 
highest evaluation scores demonstrated significantly higher citations, whereas lower-scored papers showed no such correlation with 
citation impact. This indicates that reviewers possess greater accuracy in identifying excellent papers. Similarly, Patterson & Harris 
(2009) conducted an investigation using reviewer scores from manuscripts submitted to Physics in Medicine and Biology, discovering 
that publications classified in the highest quality category received approximately double the citations. These findings demonstrate 
that reviewer scores correlate with citation impact primarily for high-quality papers, while other studies present evidence showing that 
manuscripts receiving lower peer reviewers consistently attract relatively fewer citations when published. For instance, study by Siler 
et al (2015) demonstrated this relationship using comprehensive data from both published and rejected manuscripts across three elite 
medical journals. In the same vein, numerous studies across diverse research domains have employed publicly available datasets to 
demonstrate consistent positive correlations between higher reviewer scores and increased citations (Bornmann, Marx, Schier, Thor, & 
Daniel, 2010).
Despite those positive findings, contrasting evidence has emerged from other investigations, which report that manuscripts with 
greater inconsistency in review scores subsequently garnered more citations (Xie et al., 2024), or higher citations or no significant 
association between review scores and eventual citation performance (Bartneck, 2017). A potential reason for the inconsistent findings 
may lie in the fact that the existing review scores are self-assessed by the reviewers themselves. These scores represent a perceived 
measure of research quality and only reflects the quality of the review itself. Moreover, such measurement is subject to inherent 
limitations, including subjective issues, inconsistent among reviewers (Bharti et al., 2022), and a tendency for junior reviewers to 
assign lower scores (Wu et al., 2024). In this regard, a more precise and objective approach to evaluating reviewer performance, for 
example, by employing RQI in a more objective and accountable manner is needed. However, the traditional RQI evaluation process is 
dependent on humans, suffers from human errors, and is time-consuming.
2.2. LLMs for peer review
To address the aforementioned challenges in evaluating peer review quality more effectively and objectively, LLMs demonstrate 
considerable promise. LLMs are the emerging artificial intelligence technologies trained on vast amounts of data from multiple sources 
to generate human-like responses. Since the first widely released LLM (ChatGPT) by OpenAI in 2022, numerous LLMs have been 
developed by various companies and institutions to accomplish multiple tasks in both industry and academia (Clusmann et al., 2023; 
Luo et al., 2025). They have showed promised in peer review relevant tasks, such as being used as checklist assistants during the 
pre-peer review stage to aid editors-in-chief in checking whether submitted manuscripts align well with the submission guidelines and 
academic standards of journals. In this type of task, existing literature demonstrated that LLMs exhibited potential in identifying errors 
in submissions and achieving higher accuracy in confirming submission checklists (Goldberg et al., 2024; Liu & Shah, 2023).
Notably, considerable research attention has centered on the capacity of LLMs aiding in reshaping the peer review process. Multiple 
investigations have examined potential LLM applications in simulating the dynamic and iterative nature of peer review and in 
automated review generation and assign review scores (Liang et al., 2024; Tan et al., 2024). LLMs such as GPT-3.5, PaLM2, and 
LLaMA2 have been demonstrated to outperform humans in generating reviews (Santu et al., 2024), succeed in offering critical 
comments (Tunga & Çelik, 2024), and provide sentiment analysis to predict paper decisions (Srivastava, 2023). Moreover, LLMs have 
shown the ability to produce weak to moderately strong predictions of peer review scores (Thelwall & Yaghi, 2024), and have been 
observed to be efficient in detecting gender and geographic bias in peer review (Sebo, 2024). Despite the significant potential of LLMs 
in aiding peer review, concerns regarding inherent biases and ethical risks emphasize that LLMs should serve as supportive tools rather 
than substitutes for human evaluators. The validation of LLM-generated results by humans and well-designed prompts are essential to 
ensure the quality and accuracy of LLM outputs (Huang et al., 2025).
In summary, given the inherent flaws of employing self-assessed review scores as proxies for peer review quality, as well as the 
potential of LLMs when compared with human evaluations to promote more effective peer review quality assessment through well- 
developed RQI, this study aims to extend this research direction by exploring the potential of LLMs in aiding peer review quality 
assessment tasks, and ultimately presenting a more precise exploration of the relationship between peer review quality and scientific 
impact.
3. Data and methods
3.1. Data
Peer review reports: Peer review reports (PRRs) for papers were downloaded from the website of the eLife journal, covering the 
time period from 2012 that the journal’s initiation of publicizing PRRs up to 2021. I choose eLife as the target journal for the reasons 
that: 1) it advocates single-blind peer review policy by provides unprecedented access to complete PRRs; and 2) it is a well-known 
journal supported by prestigious organizations like the Howard Hughes Medical Institute (HHMI). PRRs published 2022 and after 
were excluded due to the announcement of a significant transformation in eLife’s peer review system, wherein the journal ceased 
making accept-or-reject decisions by traditional peer review, instead designating submissions as reviewed preprints (Else, 2022). As 
this change impacts the peer review process, PRRs from this period were not considered in the analysis. Additionally, only PRRs that 
included reviewers’ information were selected, as reviewer identities may influence peer review quality and thus need to be accounted 
for (McNutt et al., 1990). After excluding PRRs without reviewer information, a total of 2,580 papers, along with their corresponding 
PRRs (one entire review per paper), were collected for the study.

Review quality instrument: In biomedical research domain, validated RQIs have been developed and widely utilized to enhance 
the peer review process in well-conducted trials by assessing the quality of PPR. According to the latest review by Superchi et al. 
(2019), a total of 24 RQIs were identified. Following an in-depth screening of these instruments, those designed to evaluate quality 
domains using Likert scales, with multiple items scored from 1 (indicating poor performance) to 4 or 5 (indicating excellent quality), 
were considered suitable for quantitatively assessing review quality. Based on these selection criteria, the most prominent RQI, which 
incorporates seven items for assessing reviews, was used in this study (Van Rooyen et al., 1999).
As presented in Table 1, seven items are designed to evaluate various aspects of the reviewer’s performance, which better align with 
the reviewer instructions eLife provide for reviewer to write comments2. Specifically, the RQI assesses whether the reviewer has: (1) 
addressed the importance of the reviewed manuscript; (2) highlighted its originality; (3) identified the strengths and weaknesses of the 
methodology in the reviewed manuscript; (4) evaluated the presentation of the paper, including its writing and organization; (5) 
provided comments relevant to the result interpretation; (6) provided constructive comments; and (7) provided substantiated com­
ments. Each item is scored based on a 5-point Likert scale, and the total score is calculated as the mean of the scores across the seven 
items.
Scientific impact: The number of citations a paper receives is widely used to proxy its scientific impact (Wolfram et al., 2021). 
Generally, the more a paper is cited, the greater its presumed scientific influence. For the 2,580 collected papers, citation counts were 
retrieved using the Web of Science (WoS) database. By utilizing the digital object identifier (DOI) of each paper, three metrics 
reflecting the post-publication scientific impact were obtained and calculated: the number of citations within the first three years after 
publication, the average citations within this period, and the “year-normalised” citations. The normalisation was performed using the 
average citations received by all “biology” publications within the three years following their publication.
3.2. LLMs and prompt development
LLMs: The LLMs utilized was Gemini 2.0 Flash (Google, 2024), the latest version developed by Google. Gemini 2.0 Flash has 
demonstrated exceptional performance in answering complex questions and providing detailed explanations of its “thinking” process. 
This capability to articulate its reasoning makes it particularly well-suited for tasks involving peer review quality assessment. In this 
study, the systematic evaluation of each item in the RQI requires not only accurate judgments but also a clear rationale for those 
judgments. The model’s ability to simulate a structured decision-making process aligns well with the critical thinking required to assess 
the review quality effectively.
Few-Shot Prompt: Output performance of LLMs often depends on effective prompt engineering, to guide the model to mimic a 
natural conversational process. Specifically, I employed the widely used few-shot prompting technique to elicit Gemini 2.0 Flash’s 
feedback for the review quality assessment task. Few-shot prompt provides contextual reasoning before assigning scores of each input 
PPR.
As the prompt template presented in Fig. 1, two important factors that align with the PPR context of papers in eLife were considered 
in constructing the few-shot prompt approach. First, the single-blind peer review practices employed by the eLife journal was simu­
lated. Second, given the token limitations of Gemini 2.0 Flash as noted in existing work (Huang et al., 2025), I represented the original 
content of each paper using its abstract to capture the core manuscript content while having Gemini 2.0 Flash assume the role of a 
reviewer. Specifically, as shown in Fig. A1-A3, three examples representing high-, medium-, and low-quality review were used to guide 
Gemini 2.0 Flash in contextual reasoning.
Furthermore, three components that make up the benchmark prompt were included: 1) task description to clearly inform the LLM 
of Gemini 2.0 Flash specific task of review quality assessment (as presented in Fig. 1); 2) instructions of RQI items that providing 
Gemini 2.0 Flash a detailed explanation of the items in the RQI used to assess the quality of peer reviews (as presented in Fig. A4); and 
3) query for task evaluation to specify the desired format of the output, including (a) scores for each RQI item as shown in Table 1, (b) a 
textual summary highlighting the strengths of the reviewed manuscript, (c) constructive comments for improvement, and (d) an 
explanation of the scores assigned by the model (as presented in Fig. A5).
The response output of the Gemini 2.0 Flash is structured in a well-organized JSON format, making it more suitable for further 
analysis. Considering that zero-shot prompt directly instructs Gemini 2.0 Flash to assess review quality without additional contextual 
information, the results were also included as a supplementary reference.
3.3. Reliability analysis and empirical strategy
LLMs results evaluation: To examine the validity of the results generated by Gemini 2.0 Flash, I conducted a reliability analysis 
involving two human evaluators who manually assessed the review quality of a subsample of 300 papers. Prior to the formal evaluation 
tasks, the evaluators underwent thorough training to ensure their consistency. To this end, I first make sure that they were familiar 
with the detailed explanations of each item for review quality assessments. Then a practice of evaluating the review quality by using 10 
additional papers outside the sample was conducted and repeated to ensure they achieved excellent agreement before the formal 
evaluation.

4. Results
The results are presented in the following three steps. First, the reliability of Gemini 2.0 Flash in assessing review quality by 
comparing its performance with human evaluators through a subsample analysis is tested. Then, the statistical properties of the review 
quality scores generated by Gemini 2.0 in the dataset is presented. Lastly and importantly, the core analysis of exploring the rela­
tionship between review scores assessed by Gemini 2.0 and citations is conducted.
4.1. Reliability analysis of Gemini 2.0 Flash in evaluating review quality
The results of human evaluators and Gemini 2.0 Flash’s output for the subsample are presented. Since each item in the RQI is scored 
on a scale of 1 to 5 by both human and Gemini 2.0 Flash evaluators, I calculate the average score for each item across the 300 sub­
sample papers. As shown in Fig. 2, Gemini 2.0 Flash scored comparably to human evaluators on item 2 (importance of the study, as 
outlined in Table 1). This similarity is further reflected in the mean and standard deviation values of 3.51 (0.93) and 3.29 (1.14), 
respectively (Table 2), confirming that scores assigned by both evaluators were comparable. Scores for all remaining items demon­
strated consistent patterns (Fig. 2 and Table 2).
To evaluate the reliability between the two evaluators, the agreement of the evaluated item scores is calculated. Results in Table 2
show that all item scores achieve observed agreement values are around 0.99, implying excellent agreement between the two 
evaluators.
To further validate the consistent performance of human evaluators and Gemini 2.0 Flash’s output, the agreement results for the 
average overall score of the review are presented in Table 34. The values are 0.86 and 0.79, suggesting that the performance of using 
Gemini 2.0 Flash for review quality assessment is comparable to the human evaluators. The excellent agreement is observed, further 
supporting the alignment between the human and automation evaluation of PPRs. For the Gemini 2.0 Flash constituency with two 
repetitions, an excellent agreement of 0.98 is also observed.
4.2. Statistics of review scores generated by Gemini 2.0 Flash
An analysis of the responses generated by Gemini 2.0 Flash is conducted, focusing on analyzing reviewers’ roles in the peer review 
process to assess review quality (Gendler Szab´o, 2001). As shown in Fig. 3, the score distribution generated by Gemini 2.0 Flash in­
dicates that the scores for the seven items predominantly range between 3 and 5.
4.3. Relationship between review quality and citations
To examine the relationship between review scores assigned by Gemini 2.0 Flash and the scientific impact of a paper, measured by 
the number of citations within the first three years after publication, the results of the linear regression analysis are reported in Column 
1 of Table 4. The estimated coefficient is 5.440 and is statistically significant at the 1% level, suggesting a positive association between 
a paper’s review score and its subsequent scientific impact. Specifically, a one-point increase in the review score (reflecting higher- 
quality peer review) corresponds to an average increase of approximately five citations.
Several robustness checks are conducted to further validate the estimated effect. First, to address potential heteroscedasticity, the 
dependent variable (number of citations) is transformed into its logarithmic form using the formula: ln(citations). The results, present 
in Column 2 of Table 4, demonstrate consistency with the baseline estimation, as the coefficient remains positive and statistically 
significant. Then, to mitigate the influence of outliers, papers with an exceptionally high number of citations are excluded and re- 
estimate the benchmark model. Specifically, papers ranking in the top 1% by citations are removed, and the result are reported in 
Column 3 of Table 4. The coefficient for review quality remains positive, with the magnitude and significance consistent with those from 
the baseline model. Finally, as a further robustness check, I account for the count data nature of the number of citations by specifying a 
negative binomial regression model to address potential non-linearity in the data. The corresponding results are presented in Column 4 
of Table 4. The signs, magnitudes, and statistical significance of the coefficients were similar to those in the baseline model, suggesting 
that the observed effect is robust across alternative model specifications.

citations and “year-normalised” citations as dependent variables, the findings shown in Table A2 were consistent with the main result 
displayed in Table 4. Second, when replicating the analytical process using a zero-shot prompt to automatically assess the review 
scores, the positive effect persisted (Figs. A6 and A7, and Table A3). Third, when further considering first-author information to proxy

5. Discussion
Given the substantial increase in the volume of scientific publications, it is crucial to examine whether peer review effectively 
safeguards research quality, and therefore enhancing the scientific impact. Utilizing the latest version of Google’s LLMs, Gemini 2.0 
Flash, I automatically assess the quality of peer reviews in biomedical papers using the RQI and analyzed the number of citations 
within the first three years after publication as a proxy for scientific impact. Findings indicate that high-quality peer reviews are 
positively associated with the subsequent scientific influence of the reviewed papers. Specifically, the estimated coefficient is positive 
and statistically significant (5.440, p < 0.01), suggesting that a one-point increase in review score, reflecting the peer review quality 
assessed by Gemini 2.0 Flash, corresponds to an average of 5.440 additional citations. When using both average citations and the “year- 
normalised” citations as dependent variables, the positive effect remain persist. The results imply that reviewers who provide high- 
quality feedback may contribute to improving the quality of submissions, thereby increasing their recognition within the scientific 
community and ultimately leading to higher citation counts.

This study benefits from the rapid advancements in the development of LLMs and their growing academic attention in the eval­
uation of tasks related to peer review. Existing research has assessed the quality of peer review feedback generated by LLMs, such as 
GPT-4, and compared it with feedback provided by human evaluators (Liang et al., 2024). The findings indicate that GPT-4’s feedback 
significantly overlaps with that of human reviewers. Notably, in the case of ICLR papers, the overlap reached as high as 77.18%, 
providing fundamental evidence for the LLMs’ potential in automatically assessing peer review quality. Different from their research 
design, this study builds on the use of most recent LLM of Gemini 2.0 Flash, which exhibit promising capabilities in answering complex 
questions and offering detailed explanations of the “thinking” process. Additionally, this study exclusively employs the RQI, a widely 
recognized tool in assessing the quality of peer reviews within the biomedical research domain. These methodological choices enhance 
the credibility of the evidence presented in this study.
To further strengthen the claim that the review scores automatically evaluated by Gemini 2.0 Flash are reliable and comparable to 
those of human evaluators, I conduct an analysis by using a subsample of 300 PPRs. This analysis compares the evaluation performance 
of Gemini 2.0 Flash with that of two human evaluators. The human evaluators undergo thorough training to ensure they are qualified 
to perform the evaluation tasks. The results of the analysis reveal excellent agreement between the automatic and human evaluations 
of peer review quality. The observed agreement is chose because of the narrow range of Likert-scale scores, where kappa or correlation 
analysis cannot capture the substantial consistency of a difference of 1 (Rothwell & Martyn, 2000).The finding suggests that LLMs, such 
as Gemini 2.0 Flash, have the potential to alleviate the time-consuming process of evaluating peer review quality (Thelwall, 2024a), 
and exhibit potential that LLMs might be useful for journal editors, conference organizers, or other parties interested in assessing the 
quality of peer reviews. However, the findings should be regarded as an initial effort in assessing the performance of Gemini 2.0 Flash 
in peer review quality evaluation. Further research and development are necessary to fully establish its effectiveness and reliability in 
this domain, given the ongoing controversy and ethical concerns surrounding the use of LLMs for automated peer review related tasks.

published research, it is important to acknowledge that other factors, such as the inherent novelty or significance of the research, may 
also influence scientific impact. Research quality remains a multifaceted concept, and there is still no consensus on how to define or

have been proposed to assess research quality (Arts et al., 2021; Funk & Owen-Smith, 2017; Leibel & Bornmann, 2024; Wu & Wu, 
2019; Wu & Yan, 2019; Wu et al., 2019), these are often citation-based and suffer from the inherent limitations of citation alone is not

Limitations
This study inevitably has limitations. First and most importantly, LLMs raise significant concerns regarding bias (Ye et al., 2024), as 
they are trained on vast and varied datasets source where intergroup prejudice is often pervasive. Therefore, the growing reliance on 
LLMs to replace human judgment in assessing peer review quality requires more in-depth analysis (Bail, 2024; (Goldberg et al., 2024; 
Liu & Shah, 2023). Second, due to resource limitations, only two types of prompts (few-shot prompt and zero-shot prompt) are utilized 
to guide LLMs in generating responses (Reynolds & McDonell, 2021). Fine-tuning prompt techniques to enhance the accuracy and 
efficiency of LLM performance warrant further exploration (Sahoo et al., 2024). Also, when without resource constraints, using a 
relatively larger sample size to verify the conclusions of this study is recommended for future research. Third, the review scores 
assessed by Gemini 2.0 Flash are based on a 5-point Likert scale. The limited range of this scale may contribute to the lack of variation 
observed across the seven items. The potential impact of this constraint warrants further investigation. Finally, this analysis is limited 
to PPRs in the life sciences and biomedical domains by using the well-established RQI tool in a unique journal. It is important to 
highlight that other factor, such as research significance, may also influence scientific impact but were not explicitly included as review 
quality items in the current RQI. Generalizing the findings to a broader range of research disciplines with additional items to address 
review quality is needed.
6. Conclusions
This study utilizes Google’s most recent LLM, Gemini 2.0 Flash, to automate the evaluation of peer review quality for biomedical 
research using the widely recognized RQI tool. And the relationship between automatically assessed review scores and scientific 
impact is examined. The results demonstrate that Gemini 2.0 Flash provides reliable and comparable evaluations to those of human 
reviewers for PPRs, highlighting its potential for large-scale PPRs evaluation. Further linear regression analysis reveals a positive 
association between review scores automatically assessed by Gemini 2.0 Flash and subsequent citations. Specifically, a one-point 
increase in the review score corresponds to an average increase of approximately five citations. Several robustness checks, 
including transforming citation counts into their logarithmic form, excluding papers within the top 1% of citations, and employing 
negative binomial regression model, confirm the robustness of the estimated results. These findings underscore the promise of LLMs in 
the automatic assessment of peer review quality; however, further efforts are needed to ensure their reliability and enhance their 
application across diverse research domains.
